    The Microphysiology Systems Database (MPS-Db) is key
    for the development and application of MPS

    The MPS-Db aggregates MPS data with linked preclinical and clinical databases for analysis in the context of human exposure. Built in tools enable the assessment of the reproducibility and transferability of MPS models, while computational models are being developed to enhance the value of MPS models for understanding disease mechanisms, compound toxicity, and prediction of drug pharmacokinetics (PK) (Gough et al. 2016).

    • An Icon-Driven Interface Provides Intuitive Workflow


      An icon-driven workflow guides the user in selecting study components (A), creating and reviewing studies (B), analyzing data (C), and performing computational modeling (D).

    • Learn About MPS Models Being Used in Translational Medicine


      The MPS Models page provides a list of models being used by researchers working at the leading edge of the field. Selecting View next to an MPS model shows detailed information on a model's design and construction.

      Click here to see a list of institutions with models in the MPS-Db.

    • Add and Analyze Experimentally Derived Assay Study Data


      Design and add a study to the MPS-Db, add chip treatment information, then upload assay data and use the online tools to visualize, analyze, and computationally model the results. The database includes a comprehensive interface for filtering and grouping data. Data can be grouped by user selected parameters to facilitate comparisons, such as treated vs untreated tissue chips, and filters can be applied to limit data included in visualizations or used in analysis and computational modeling. The MPS-Db accommodates assay data from all types of MPS organ models and assays (e.g., biosensors, ELISA, high content imaging, mass spec analysis).

    • Controlled Sharing of Experimentally Derived Assay Study Data


      Researchers providing experimental data determine when and with whom study data are shared. Data can be kept private, shared within an institution or with a small group of collaborators, opened to the public, and/or opened through a process that includes an embargo period.

      Click here to see a list of studies that are scheduled to be released in the next {{ number_of_days }} days.

    • Reproducibility Metrics



      Reproducibility and transferability of MPS experimental models can be assessed using the reproducibility analysis tool. The tool can be accessed from within a single study to assess the intra-study reproducibility, or through the (Inter-Study Graphing/Reproducibility) feature to assess reproducibility across studies and/or across laboratories.

    • Search Drug Trial Information



      Review Drug Trial information, aggregated together from multiple sources, presented in a searchable and sortable table. Click View to select a specific drug trial and learn more about the results of the trial and the source of the data.

    • Adverse Events


      Using data from the FDA Adverse Event Reporting System (FAERS) and the CDC's National Ambulatory Medical Care Survey (NAMCS), the Adverse Events feature presents reported adverse events in a sortable and searchable table. Search for a compound, then order by number of reported events to evaluate potential test compounds.

    • Compare Adverse Drug Related Events


      Using data from the FDA Adverse Event Reporting System (FAERS) and the CDC's National Ambulatory Medical Care Survey (NAMCS), the Compare Adverse Events feature enables quick comparison of reported adverse events associated to multiple compounds. Sort and search tools facilitate rapid identification of compounds and events, and selections are made by simply checking boxes.

    • View Compound Summaries and Targets


      Access the Compound List page to review compound information, including LogP, molecular weight, and compound synonyms, presented in a searchable and sortable table. Click the View button next to a compound for additional information, including summaries of pre-clinical and clinical findings and targets associated with the compound. Review data sources in the Overview of Database Features table of the Help page.

    • Learn About a Disease and Studies in the MPS-Db Relating to a Disease



      The Disease Portal summarizes the number of studies in the MPS-Db relating to a specific disease, and provides links with additional information about the disease, including a Disease Overview, Disease Biology, Clinical Data associated with the disease, and links to Disease Models & Studies studies relating to the disease.

    • Find Information Quickly Using the Global Search Function


      The Global Search function, available on the Home page, simplifies the process of finding information. Enter a compound, a disease, a device, or other key word(s) (e.g., Cmax) in the global search box and click Search to quickly locate matching content in the database.

    Frequently Asked Questions

    Registration is easy. Just go to the MPS Login page and click on the option to register, or just click here.
    The MPS-Db Help page provides detailed information and step-by-step examples on how to use the features of the MPS-Db.
    Yes. The database offers multiple ways of looking at compound data. Chemical, biological, and clinical information is available through the Compounds icon. The Global Search feature can also be helpful for finding information about a specific compound.
    All use of the MPS-Db and content is free for non-profit research applications provided any published works reference the MPS Database website. The current version of the code is also available for non-profit use and can be downloaded from GitHub. All registered users have free access to the public content and tools; however use of the data or tools in for-profit applications requires a license. The MPS-Db code will be made available to corporations for internal use for a nominal fee to help support maintenance and extension of the MPS-Db. The publicly accessible content of the MPS-DB can also be provided as a PostgreSQL database to nonprofit organizations, and for a license fee to for-profit corporations. For more information, send an email to MPSHELP@pitt.edu with "For-profit Use of the MPS-Db" in the subject line.
    To add assay data to the database, you must be a registered user, assigned to a Data Group. Contact an administrator by sending an email to MPSHELP@pitt.edu with "Getting Set Up to Contribute Data to the MPS-Db" in the subject line. Detailed information on how to add, review and analyze data in the MPS-Db is available on the database's Help page.
    Data contributors are not required by the MPS-Db Center to share their data. Studies that have been shared publicly, or for which you are designated as a Data Group member or collaborator, will show on the Study List page of the database. These studies can also be accessed through the inter-study analysis feature and the disease portal, and when reviewing the Target/Analytes and Method/Kits lists (available through the Studies->Study Components menu). Review the Help page to learn about these features.
    Data “stakeholders” are designated by the data contributor as users that have a vested interest in the MPS Model or data, such as a model developer. Stakeholders have the opportunity to review studies for which they have been designated as a stakeholder, following sign off by the data contributor and prior to the data being made accessible to any other user groups. To review these studies, stakeholders must be registered MPS-Db users and granted permission to review and acknowledge that you have reviewed the relevant study data. The study (or studies) to be reviewed can be found on the Study List page. Look for studies with a check mark, but without an ‘eye’ or a ‘pencil’ icon in the Review column.
    The MPS-Db is funded by the National Center for Advancing Translational Sciences Tissue Chips Program, the National Center for Advancing Translational Sciences (NCATS) Tissue Chip Testing Centers Program, and in part by the Vanderbilt-Pittsburgh Resource for Organotypic Models for Predictive Toxicology.
